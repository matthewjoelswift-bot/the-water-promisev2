The daily reality behind a cup

It's easy to let "clean water" wash over you as an abstraction, so here's what it actually means. Roughly 1 in 4 people worldwide — about 2.2 billion — still lack safely managed drinking water (WHO and UNICEF, 2025). For many of them, "getting water" is not a tap but a walk: often hours a day, usually done by women and girls, to a source that may still make their kids sick. That walk is time not spent in school, or working, or resting. When a village gets a clean source close to home, the change isn't only medical — it hands people back their hours. That reclaimed time is a lot of what your cups are really buying.

How the work actually gets done

We don't show up in a community with a drill, and charity: water doesn't either. They fund and support local partner organizations who know the place, choose the technology that fits — a well, a piped system, a rainwater catchment, a filter — and stay accountable for it long after the ribbon-cutting. Every funded project is logged with GPS coordinates and photos, so "we built a well" is a thing you can point at on a map rather than take on faith.
