Where the water comes from

Every run pours a little water.

We don't drill wells. charity: water does — with a 20-year track record and public proof. Our job is to turn attention into money and send it their way, honestly and transparently.

The 100% model

charity: water sends 100% of public donations to the field — they even cover the card-processing fees — because their operating costs are funded separately by private backers and partners. That's why we can promise that the money we raise for water actually becomes water.

How a run becomes a cup

When you finish a run, the page refreshes its ads. Advertisers pay for those views. We take a share of that revenue and donate it — enough to fund at least one cup of clean water per run. The gap between what an ad earns and what a cup costs covers the small costs of running and growing this, so more people play and more water gets funded over time.

Transparency

We'll publish a live ledger of what's been donated and the receipts behind it. The counters here show cups and dollars given — the impact — and the completion proof (GPS and photos) comes straight from charity: water's own reporting.

From a cup to a whole community

A "cup" is deliberately the smallest honest unit we could pick — 250 ml, about $0.00023 of funded water. It's small on purpose, because it maps to a single finished run. But the cups ladder up. charity: water's own figure is that it costs roughly $40 to bring one person clean water, and an average project runs about $10,524 and serves a whole community for years. So the same math that says "one run, one cup" also says: a few hundred runs is a person's water for a day, tens of thousands is a person served for good, and enough of us typing together is a well. Nothing here is rounded up — it's the same rate, just viewed at different zoom levels.

Built to pay for itself

For any of this to last, it has to keep running without a fundraiser propping it up. That's the quiet advantage of the ad-funded loop: a viewed ad tends to earn more than a cup of water costs, and that small gap is what covers hosting, tools, and the work of reaching more people. We're not trying to skim — we're trying to build something that funds water indefinitely because more people keep showing up to get faster. The better the game, the more water. Those incentives point the same way, on purpose.
