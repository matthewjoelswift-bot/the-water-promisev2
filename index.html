How a run becomes water

The whole thing is one honest loop, short enough to explain in a breath. You play a round and get a little faster. Finishing the round refreshes an ad on the page, and advertisers pay for that view. We take a share of what the ad earns and send it to charity: water, who turns it into clean water in the field. You came for the typing practice; the water got funded because you showed up. Nobody had to donate a cent.

Step one

Play a round

A minute of the adaptive typing coach on your keyboard, or a quick word puzzle on your phone. You come back because you're genuinely getting faster — that's the point.

Step two

An ad refreshes

Finishing a round refreshes the ad on the page. Advertisers pay for real, viewed impressions — so the money that does the work never comes out of your pocket.

Step three

A cup is funded

We route a share of that ad revenue to charity: water. At their rates, one finished run covers at least one real cup of clean water — a true number, with receipts to follow.

Why we built it this honest way

Plenty of "play a game, save the world" projects quietly fall apart when you look closely — the impact turns out to be a vague gesture, or the game isn't worth playing once the novelty wears off. We wanted the opposite on both counts.

So the game had to earn its place on your screen. It's a real typing coach built on how people actually get faster (there's a whole page on the method), not a stopwatch with a nice skin. And the impact had to be a number we could defend. "Cups" isn't a feel-good abstraction — it's a unit for the dollars raised, priced off charity: water's published rates, and every cup we claim will be backed by what we actually gave. If we can't prove it, we won't say it.

A problem that's actually solvable

Roughly 1 in 4 people worldwide — about 2.2 billion — still lack safely managed drinking water (WHO and UNICEF, 2025). It's an enormous number, but water is one of the most tractable problems in global development: the engineering is known, the cost per person is measurable, and a single well can change daily life for a whole village. charity: water has spent nearly two decades funding that work with local partners and a public, GPS-and-photo record of every project. We don't drill wells or run programs — we're a small on-ramp that turns a few minutes of typing into money for the people who do.
